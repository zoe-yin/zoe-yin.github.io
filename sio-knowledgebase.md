# SIO Geophysics Knowledgebase

This is a short doc with info about being a geophysics student at SIO. There is a lot of informal knowledge that gets passed around within the geophysics program. This doc attempts to formalize some of that knowledge to improve student outcomes and equalize access to informal knowledge.

## Acronyms
**SIO** - Scripps Institution of Oceanography
**GP** - Usually refers to the geophysics curricular group
**IGPP** - Institute for Geophysics and Planetary Physics: the institute within SIO where the geophysics department resides. I don't know all the details of the administrative differences but I think IGPP refers more to the research arm and GP refers more to the academic arm.
**TG** - Short for TGIF (Thank God it's Friday), TG is a donation-based event on Fridays with free beer.  

## New GP Students
- Most students arrive around August of the year that they begin. 
- Each new student gets a laptop paid for by the Green Foundation (an endowment that funds several activities), usually a relatively basic MacBook (exact specs vary year to year). Advisors can fund upgrades (not sure exactly how this works, ask your advisor if you think you'll need more computing power for your work).

## Program Requirements
- You must register for 12 credits every quarter, except summer quarters. Regular classes can contribute to this 12 hr minimum but if you are not take enough or any classes, then you register for SIO 299, which is research credits. When you register (with [WebReg](https://act.ucsd.edu/webreg2)) you choose your advisor as the instructor of the 299 credits.

## Advisor-student Relationship
-  Advisor-student dynamics are not very formalized and it is largely up to you to set reasonable expectations with your advisor and come to a mutual understanding of what is acceptable and what is not. It is a great idea to have an open conversation about expectations before starting work with your advisor and revisit that conversation throughout your graduate studies. 
- Unfortunately, there is an inherent power dynamic that exists in the advisor/student relationship and, as such, some small portion of advisors will inevitably abuse that power. There are some rare people as Scripps who have repeatedly had conflicts with students and might require some extra consideration before working with them. Get in touch with me (hyin@ucsd.edu) or other grad students if you'd like to have a open conversation about this if you are a prospective student. The student body is actively pushing the SIO administration to create some better checks on abusive or toxic advising behavior and hope that this will improve in the future so that these warnings won't be necessary. That's not how things should be, especially at a leading institution like Scripps. If you are not sure what to make of what others have told you about your prospective advisor when considering working with them, try talking to graduate students *_not_* advised by that advisor. They often will have good insights without the complication of worrying about saying something negative about the person that they work for.
- If you are struggling with a relationship with your advisor, feel free to reach out to me or to other geophysics graduate students. You need not view your advisor as "toxic" or "abusive" to have problems that might need some extra help or advice. 
- If something doesn't seem right to you, DOCUMENT IT! You don't have to report it right then and there or at all, but if the situation escalates and you end up needing or wanting to report it through any channel, then having documentation will make reporting much easier. Again, this should not be this way that it is, but it's good advice for operating in an imperfect system. 
- If you are struggling to make sense of complicated reporting systems or where to begin with filing a grievance, one great resource is the SIO Director of Diversity Initiatives Keiara Auzenne. This type of conflict does not fall directly under her job description but she is an outstanding advocate for students who also knows all of the people and systems at SIO and UCSD and, in my opinion, would be an excellent advocate if navigating a complicated situation. 

Good questions to ask your advisor before or at the start of working with them:
 - What are your typical work hours? When do you expect me to be available to you or others in the lab? My typical schedule is _______. Does that seems reasonable to you? 
 - How long do you expect my PhD/Masters to take? What is your standard "bar" for advancing to candidacy and defending? 
 - How often would you like to meet? Would you like to have a standing weekly meeting (this is typical) or would you prefer some other arrangement? 
 - What is your policy on time off? Do you want me to clear vacations with you in advance? Do you have any limits or expectations on how much time off I should be taking? I typically like to ______, will that be okay with you? (Maybe this is "I usually go home for the holidays and take about a week off" or "I would like to go back to my home country and work remotely for a few weeks every year"). 

**_NOTE: This is not like a typical job and your advisor's answers to these questions will probably be some version of, "it depends". The purpose of asking these questions is mostly to get a sense of how they think through these issues, to give you some idea of what they think is reasonable, and to give you a starting point for future conversations, especially if conflict arises._
